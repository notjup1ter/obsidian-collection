Course Description

How do self-driving cars navigate on the road, or chatbots (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, ...) determine what to say? At the core of the technological transformation is the increasing ability of machines to learn, reason, and interact intelligently with complex data, humans, and the world. Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the core technology behind this transformation, allowing everything from our mobile phones and smart homes to robots and medical diagnostic tools to understand complex information and act intelligently.
But AI is about so much more. Have you ever wondered: Can machines truly learn, understand, and even create? What separates today's AI from mere automation? And how do we ensure these intelligent technologies serve humanity ethically? This course will explore and apply the core concepts of AI, from intelligent agents and search to machine learning and generative AI. You'll gain hands-on experience through class exercises and homework assignments. You'll solidify your understanding of AI concepts with weekly review questions and midterms. A significant component of this course is the project, where you'll have the opportunity to design, implement, and evaluate an AI system for a problem you found at UVA/Charlottesville.
Prerequisite: CS 2150 or CS 3100 with a grade of C- or better.

Course Format

The course will be delivered through lectures. In addition to two lectures a week, students will have weekly review questions, 5 programming homework assignments, two midterm exams, and a final project to practice the concepts presented in the lectures.

Textbook (optional)

Details and grading policy

Weekly review questions: 5%
Weekly review questions will help you review the key concepts of lectures and course material. Review questions will be offered at the end of most weeks in a multiple-choice format on Gradescope.
You can review them at your own pace after class. Questions will be available for a week, no extension. Only your top five scores will be used in the final grade.
Participation: 5%
As AI is evolving very quickly, we encourage you to have continuous participation within and outside of lectures in any format to keep engaging in or learning about AI. This includes, but is not limited to, the following activities.
In-class activities (responses submitted through online forms), AI news/tips sharing (up to two students each lecture), Piazza discussions (including questions and answering about AI, not logistics), scribe lectures (up to two scribers per lecture), and participate in AI activities (hackathons, talks/seminars, etc.) outside of class.
You can participate in any format that works best for you. At least ten activities are needed to have full participation credit in the final grade.
Homework: 30%
Homework assignments are designed to provide you with individual hands-on practice to translate theoretical knowledge into working code (in Python). You will be implementing various types of AI agents to play a game like Minesweeper! In each assignment, we provide you with a description of agent design and skeleton code. You will apply the foundational concepts and critical thinking you learned to complete the AI agents.
Late submission is allowed for three days after the deadline with a 20% deduction per day. We will automatically grant two slip days for each student throughout the semester.
Slip day: Using slip days avoids the late penalty. You can use your two slip days in one homework (two days) or two homeworks (one day each homework).
Midterm: 30%
The midterm exams will serve as a comprehensive assessment to check your understanding of core AI concepts and methods. It will be an open-note exam, including multiple-choice questions and short-answer questions. Questions will focus on the concept, algorithm, and simple calculations.
Course project: 30%
The course project challenges you to integrate various AI concepts into a cohesive solution for a real-world problem, a problem you found in UVA/Charlottesville! Working in teams, you will navigate the full problem-solving lifecycle, from identifying appropriate AI methods and leveraging libraries to researching relevant resources. This hands-on experience is critical for developing the confidence and practical skills needed to tackle complex AI challenges beyond the classroom.

Final project guidelines

Topic: Solving problems in UVA or Charlottesville with AI
Identify a problem you or students face in UVA/Charlottesville, design an AI agent, and apply the AI or generative AI methods taught in the class to implement the designed AI agent to solve the problem.

Generative AI Policy

In this course, we will explore different topics in AI, and of course, how to use generative AI tools is of interest. We also have some opportunities for you to practice with these tools. I encourage you to explore generative AI tools to help your learning, but very importantly, use them with a critical and responsible view. That means
DO NOT simply copy the review questions, homework assignments, and lecture videos to ask AI to generate answers and code to submit without working on them yourself.
DO NOT use AI to directly generate reports without thinking about the project and working on it yourself.
DO discuss with AI to clarify or explain the concepts you do not understand well, and if uncertain, ask and discuss on Piazza or during office hours!
DO brainstorm ideas with AI, but critically think about whether the ideas make sense or how you may use or implement them.
DO use AI to improve your writing, such as proofreading, grammar/spellchecking, suggesting organization of content, etc, but review them to learn what is edited and if you agree with them.
The goal of using AI is to help you learn and provide you with more ways to learn at your own pace, but the goal is not to outsource your brain to AI and not to do your coursework (this is exactly replacing yourself with an AI!)
If you use any generative AI tools in your homework assignments and projects in any way, you are required to acknowledge and cite all generative AI use/contributions.
Create an appendix at the end of your submission with the following information:
The name of the platforms you used
The exact prompts or conversations you had with AI
The raw AI output (if it answers)
Any iterations or adaptations you have to improve the outputs
Explain how you use the output or what you find the AI fell short on

We are fortunate to have an SDAC advisor, Courtney MacMasters, physically located in Engineering. You may email her at cmacmasters@virginia.edu to schedule an appointment. It is the University's long-standing policy and practice to reasonably accommodate students so that they do not experience an adverse academic consequence when sincerely held religious beliefs or observances conflict with academic requirements.

Harassment, Discrimination, and Interpersonal Violence

The University of Virginia is dedicated to providing a safe and equitable learning environment for all students. If you or someone you know has been affected by power-based personal violence, more information can be found on the UVA Sexual Violence website that describes reporting options and resources available - www.virginia.edu/sexualviolence.
The same resources and options for individuals who experience sexual misconduct are available for discrimination, harassment, and retaliation.  UVA prohibits discrimination and harassment based on age, color, disability, family medical or genetic information, gender identity or expression, marital status, military status, national or ethnic origin, political affiliation, pregnancy (including childbirth and related conditions), race, religion, sex, sexual orientation, or veteran status. UVA policy also prohibits retaliation for reporting such behavior.
If you witness or are aware of someone who has experienced prohibited conduct, you are encouraged to submit a report to Just Report It (justreportit.virginia.edu) or contact EOCR, the office of Equal Opportunity and Civil Rights.
If you would prefer to disclose such conduct to a confidential resource where what you share is not reported to the University, you can turn to Counseling & Psychological Services (“CAPS”) and Women’s Center Counseling Staff and Confidential Advocates (for students of all genders).
As your professor and as a person, know that I care about you and your well-being and stand ready to provide support and resources as I can. As a faculty member, I am a responsible employee, which means that I am required by University policy and by federal law to report certain kinds of conduct that you report to me to the University's Title IX Coordinator. The Title IX Coordinator's job is to ensure that the reporting student receives the resources and support that they need, while also determining whether further action is necessary to ensure survivor safety and the safety of the University community.

Community and Identity
The Center for Connection (The Connect) is a dedicated student space within UVA Engineering that fosters academic success and personal growth. Through its programs and initiatives, The Connect helps students strengthen their engineering identity while providing resources to help them thrive during their studies and beyond. Our work centers on three key areas: student belonging and development, academic support, and community programming grounded in intentional, data-driven strategies.
The Connect features an open study area, a flexible event space, and on-site staff who provide direct support and advising to students. It is part of the Office of Community, Opportunity, and Engagement.
